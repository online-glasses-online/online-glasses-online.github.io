The Traditional Route: Your Optometrist and Your Prescription Rights

The most reliable and comprehensive method is visiting your local optometrist. A full eye exam not only determines your exact vision correction needs but also serves as a vital health check for your eyes.

When you leave the appointment, you'll need two key pieces of information: your current prescription and your pupillary distance (PD). The PD is simply the measurement between the centers of your pupils, and it's essential for ensuring your new lenses are correctly aligned for your eyes.

Don't feel pressured to buy your glasses on the spot. Thanks to the Federal Trade Commission's (FTC) Eyeglass Rule, you have the right to a copy of your prescription from your eye doctor. They are legally required to provide it to you, empowering you to shop for your spectacles wherever you find the best value.

【Decoding Your Order: Prescriptions and Lenses Explained】

Once you have your frames picked out, the next step is providing your prescription and choosing your lenses. This can feel like the most technical part of the process, but don't worry. Understanding your prescription and lens options is simple once you know what to look for.

How to Read Your Glasses Prescription: A Simple Guide

That small piece of paper from your optometrist holds all the information needed to create your perfect lenses. But what do all those numbers and abbreviations on your prescription even mean? Let's break it down.

Understanding the Numbers

Your prescription primarily consists of three values: Sphere (SPH), Cylinder (CYL), and Axis. The Sphere value indicates the lens power needed to correct nearsightedness or farsightedness. The Cylinder and Axis values work together to correct for astigmatism, which is an imperfection in the curvature of your eye.

How to measure pupillary distance for glasses

Your pupillary distance (PD) is the measurement between the centers of your pupils. It's a crucial number that ensures your lenses are correctly aligned with your eyes. While your optometrist can provide this, some at-home kits, like the one from EyeQue, include detailed instructions for measuring your PD yourself, making the online ordering process even smoother.

Understanding Lens Options and Costs for Your Affordable Eyewear

The type of lens you choose and the coatings you add will impact your final cost. However, in 2025, getting advanced features doesn't mean you have to abandon your search for cheap affordable prescription glasses.

Single-vision vs. progressive lenses cost

Single-vision lenses correct for one field of vision (either near or far) and are the most common and inexpensive option. Progressive lenses offer a seamless transition between multiple distances in one lens. While traditionally more expensive, affordable retailers like Lensabl are changing the game, offering complete pairs with either single-vision or progressive lenses starting at just $77.

Online Glasses for Astigmatism

If you have a CYL and Axis value on your prescription, you have astigmatism. The good news is that nearly all major online retailers can easily fill prescriptions that correct for astigmatism. You simply enter the numbers from your prescription, and they handle the rest.

Purchased separately at a traditional optical shop, these add-ons could easily cost an extra $200. Finding a retailer that includes these features is the secret to getting a truly great deal.
